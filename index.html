CISM Introduction

The Community Ice Sheet Model (CISM) is a next-generation ice sheet model used for predicting ice sheet evolution and sea level rise in a changing climate. This model is freely available to the glaciology and climate modeling communities. It serves as the ice dynamics component of the Community Earth System Model (CESM), which will be one of the first global climate models to include fully-coupled, dynamic ice sheets.

The starting point for CISM is the Glimmer ice sheet model developed at the University of Bristol and elsewhere. CISM has been extended to include higher-order dynamical cores ("dycores") developed by two U.S. Department of Energy projects, the Ice Sheet Initiative for Climate Extremes (ISICLES) and Predicting Ice Sheet and Climate Evolution at Extreme Scales (PISCEES). The new dycores have contributed to two major sea-level assessments, Ice2sea and SeaRISE.

Development of CISM has been through the CESM Land Ice Working Group and led out of Los Alamos National Laboratory, with contributions from other Department of Energy laboratories, including Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Sandia National Laboratories, and Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, as well as from universities including the National Center for Atmospheric Research, University of Montana, University of Colorado, New York University, University of Bristol, University of Edinburgh, Swansea University, and University of Zurich.

CISM Features

The original Glimmer model calculated mass, momentum, and energy conservation for ice sheets and glaciers using a serial, thermomechanically-coupled shallow-ice representation of ice dynamics. CISM extends the Glimmer model by adding higher-order approximations of the momentum balance (velocity solver) and adding additional physics.

CISM runs on a regular mesh using a mixture of Finite Difference and Finite Element methods and is written in Fortran90. CISM is open-source software licensed under the GNU General Public License.

CISM can be run on Mac or Linux laptops or desktops using a single or multiple processors or on supercomputing clusters with 10,000 processors or more. It has successfully been run for the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets at up to at up to 1 km horizontal resolution. CISM 2.0 is the first public release of the higher-order code. The model can be referred to as either CISM or Glimmer-CISM.

CISM 2.0 includes the following new features relative to the Glimmer 1.x model:

  • Addition of robust, parallel, 3D, 1st-order accurate Stokes momentum balance ("Glissade")
  • Addition of 3D, parallel mass and temperature transport (incremental remapping)
  • An enthalpy-based formulation for solving the conservation of energy
  • Addition of software hooks to modern C++ based solver libraries (e.g., Trilinos)
  • Replacement of Autotools build system with Cmake build system
  • Addition of new test cases (mainly for the higher-order momentum balance model)
  • Re-ordering of time step to be fully consistent with explicit forward Euler scheme (note that this changes transient SIA model answers slightly relative to previous versions of the model)
  • Addition of a new high level "cism_driver", which replaces and reproduces functionality of several old drivers (e.g., simple_glide and glint_example) and allows for more flexible integration of additional and/or external dycores
  • Re-arrangement of directory structure
  • Modifications to Glint to support coupling to CESM and other climate models that compute the surface mass balance external to the ice sheet model
  • New and updated documentation
